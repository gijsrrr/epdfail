Patient rights · GDPR / AVG · April 2026

What does your doctor know about you — and what are your rights?

Following the ChipSoft incident, many people are asking: what data was in my record, and can I find out? This page explains what healthcare providers store, what your rights are under the GDPR, and how to exercise them. You do not need a lawyer, and it costs nothing.
Background What does a healthcare provider store about you?

An Electronic Patient Record (EPD) typically contains your personal details (name, address, date of birth, BSN), your medical history, diagnoses, prescribed medications, lab results, referral letters, and notes from consultations. For specialist institutions it can also include treatment plans, imaging results, and correspondence between providers.

In the case of forensic psychiatric institutions, records may additionally include court orders, risk assessments, and forensic treatment documentation.

Under the GDPR, medical data is classified as a special category of personal data and receives the highest level of protection. Institutions are legally required to inform you if your data has been involved in a breach.

GDPR / AVG — Article 15 Your four core rights
🔍
Right of access
You can request a full copy of all data an institution holds about you. They must respond within one month, free of charge.
✏️
Right to rectification
If data about you is incorrect or incomplete, you can request a correction. The institution must act without undue delay.
🗑️
Right to erasure
In certain circumstances you can request deletion of your data. Note: healthcare providers may have a legal retention obligation (15 years for medical records under Dutch law).
📋
Right to be informed
If your data was involved in a breach, the institution is legally required to notify you. ChipSoft-related institutions are in the process of doing so.
Step by step How to submit an access request
1
Identify the right institution
Contact each institution separately — your GP, any specialist clinics, and any hospital you have visited. Each holds its own records.
2
Send a written request
Send an email or letter to the institution's privacy officer or data protection officer (DPO). Most institutions publish a dedicated privacy contact on their website. You can use the template below.
3
Verify your identity
The institution may ask you to confirm your identity — this is normal and required to protect your privacy. This can usually be done digitally via DigiD or by providing a copy of your ID with BSN redacted.
4
Wait for a response
The institution must respond within one month. In complex cases they may extend this by two additional months, but must notify you within the first month.
5
No response? File a complaint
If the institution does not respond within the deadline, you can file a complaint with the Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens at autoriteitpersoonsgegevens.nl.
Template Request letter — ready to use
Copy, fill in the highlighted fields, and send by email to the institution's privacy contact
Subject: Request for access to personal data — Article 15 GDPR Dear Sir/Madam, I am writing to exercise my right of access under Article 15 of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). I request a complete overview of all personal data you hold about me, including: - The categories of data processed - The purposes for which my data is processed - Any third parties with whom my data has been shared - The retention period for my data In light of the recent cyber incident at ChipSoft (April 2026), I would also like to know whether my personal data was affected by this incident, and if so, what specific data was involved. My details: Full name: [YOUR NAME] Date of birth: [DD/MM/YYYY] BSN: [YOUR BSN — only if required for identification] Please respond within one month as required under Article 12 GDPR. Kind regards, [YOUR NAME] [YOUR EMAIL / ADDRESS]
⏱ Timing note: Given the volume of requests institutions may currently receive following this incident, responses may come closer to the one-month deadline than usual. This is normal — the legal obligation remains.
Escalation Filing a complaint with the AP

If your institution fails to respond, or if you believe your data has been mishandled, you can file a complaint with the Autoriteit Persoonsgegevens (AP) — the Dutch data protection authority.

The AP has already received 66+ breach notifications related to the ChipSoft incident. Complaints can be filed at autoriteitpersoonsgegevens.nl/klacht-indienen.
